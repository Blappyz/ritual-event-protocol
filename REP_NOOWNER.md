# No Owner

This document explains why REP carries no license.

---

Most open source projects use a license to *grant* freedom — to say "you may copy, modify, and distribute this." The license is a legal instrument that requires a rights-holder: someone who owns the work and chooses to share it under certain terms.

REP does not use a license because REP has no rights-holder.

This is not a technicality. It is a design decision.

REP was developed to serve communities that operate outside market infrastructure. Attaching any license — even the most permissive ones — would imply that someone owns REP and has chosen to be generous. That implication would be false, and it would undermine the protocol's core claim: that coordination grammar can be ownerless.

## What This Means in Practice

- You may copy REP without asking anyone.
- You may modify REP without telling anyone.
- You may distribute REP without crediting anyone.
- You may rename REP and call it something else entirely.
- You may use REP commercially, academically, politically, or personally.
- You may not claim to own REP or any fork of it.
- You may not attach a license to REP that restricts others from doing the above.

The last two points are not legal restrictions — there is no legal mechanism to enforce them. They are protocol constraints, stated here as part of the specification. A version of REP that claims ownership or restricts forking is, by this specification, a different protocol. Call it something else.

## Why No CC0?

Creative Commons Zero (CC0) is a legal instrument that places a work in the public domain. It is an excellent tool and is used by many projects with similar intentions.

REP does not use CC0 because CC0 still requires a rights-holder to execute the waiver. Someone has to say "I own this, and I waive my rights." REP's position is that there is no one to make that statement — not because the work is unfinished, but because the work was never intended to be owned.

This is a philosophical distinction, not a practical one. In most jurisdictions, REP's materials would likely be treated similarly to CC0-licensed work. But the statement matters.

## On Forks

A community that takes REP, changes the ID prefix, adapts the card motif, translates the fields, and uses it as their own coordination tool has not violated anything. They have done exactly what REP is designed for. Their fork is their own. They own their fork. They do not own REP.

---

*This statement was written as part of REP RFC-001 and carries the same status as the rest of the specification: no owner, fork freely.*
